The Ceremony

The ceremony is the legal and emotional heart of your wedding day. Most run 20–40 minutes. Understanding your options here may be the single most important planning decision you make.

Ceremony Types
Religious, civil, humanist, interfaith — understanding your real options
The biggest decision

The type of ceremony you choose affects who can marry you, where you can legally marry, what the ceremony can include or exclude, how long it runs, and how much personalization is possible. It's worth understanding each option clearly before falling in love with a venue or officiant.

A deeper look at each type
Religious ceremonyTraditional

If one or both of you have an active faith, a ceremony within your tradition may be the most natural and meaningful choice. Religious ceremonies are conducted by ordained clergy — a priest, pastor, rabbi, imam, officiant in your denomination — and follow the structure of that faith's marriage rite. They often include scripture readings, specific prayers, and structured vows. Some faiths (particularly Catholic and Jewish ceremonies) have requirements around the other partner's faith or prior marriages, so it's worth contacting your clergy early in the planning process to understand what's expected. For LGBTQ+ couples: acceptance varies significantly across denominations and individual congregations. Many affirming clergy exist within nearly every tradition — organizations like ReconcilingWorks (Lutheran), More Light Presbyterians, and Dignity USA (Catholic) can help you find affirming officiants within your specific faith.

Civil ceremonyGrowing fast

A civil ceremony is performed by a government-authorized official — a judge, magistrate, or justice of the peace. It requires no religious affiliation whatsoever and is the legal baseline for marriage in every US state. Civil ceremonies are typically shorter and more straightforward, but can be personalized with readings, music, and personal vows depending on the officiating official. In the US, the shift toward civil ceremonies is significant: they now represent approximately 30–40% of all weddings, and that number is rising each year.

Non-denominational ceremonyMost flexible

A non-denominational ceremony is officiated by a licensed celebrant who has no requirement to follow the structure of any specific faith. It can include spiritual language, religious references, or none at all — entirely at your discretion. This is the most popular option for couples who want a meaningful, personal ceremony without being bound to one tradition. Your officiant is typically ordained through a recognized body (Universal Life Church, American Marriage Ministries, or a state-licensed celebrant program) and works with you to write a ceremony that reflects your specific story and values. These ceremonies run 20–35 minutes on average.

Humanist / secular ceremonyFastest growing

A humanist ceremony is explicitly non-religious and centers entirely on the couple — their story, their values, their community, and their commitment to each other. No deity, scripture, or supernatural element is invoked. In the US and UK, humanist ceremonies have grown dramatically over the past decade as more couples identify as non-religious or simply prefer a ceremony that feels authentic to them rather than inherited. Humanist celebrants are specially trained to create fully bespoke ceremonies and typically spend considerable time with a couple before writing anything. In Scotland, humanist ceremonies have full legal standing and are the second most popular ceremony type after civil. In England and Wales, they are not yet legally recognized as standalone ceremonies, so couples often complete legal vows separately.

Interfaith ceremonyThoughtfully complex

If you and your partner come from different faith traditions, an interfaith ceremony honors both without requiring either of you to compromise your identity. This might involve two officiants (one from each tradition), a single interfaith-trained celebrant, or a non-denominational officiant who weaves elements of both traditions into a single script. Interfaith ceremonies require more lead time — finding an officiant comfortable with both traditions, navigating family expectations, and building a structure that feels balanced and honest. They can be among the most moving ceremonies precisely because of the intentionality required.

ElopementRising in popularity

An elopement is not a lesser wedding — it's a deliberate, intimate choice. Modern elopements often involve just the couple, sometimes a small group of closest friends or family, an officiant, and a photographer. They can take place anywhere: a mountain summit, a beach at sunrise, a favorite city rooftop, or a courthouse. The legal requirements are identical to any other ceremony: a valid marriage license, a licensed officiant, and typically two witnesses. The average cost of an elopement is a fraction of a traditional wedding, and many couples report that the intimate, pressure-free experience was exactly right for them.

Symbolic / blessing ceremonyCreative use

A symbolic ceremony has no legal standing — it's a celebration of your commitment without producing a marriage certificate. This is useful for couples who legally married earlier (courthouse, abroad, in a different state) and want to hold a full celebration with friends and family, or for couples who want unlimited creative freedom in their ceremony format. The "legal" portion is handled separately and quietly; the celebration is whatever you want it to be. Nobody in attendance needs to know or care about the distinction.

Feelings & common questions

"We're not religious, but our families are." This is one of the most common tensions couples navigate. A non-denominational ceremony with a warm officiant can include language that feels spiritual and meaningful without being tied to a specific church. This often satisfies family members who want a "real" ceremony while giving you the freedom you need.

"We're from two different faiths." Interfaith ceremonies exist precisely for this. Talk to an interfaith-trained officiant early — many have experience navigating exactly this conversation with families, and they often become a quiet ally during the planning process.

"Our faith tradition doesn't fully accept our relationship." You're not alone in this, and you have real options. Many affirming officiants — including clergy — exist across most denominations. A humanist or non-denominational ceremony can also honor the spiritual meaning of your commitment without requiring a faith institution's approval. Your ceremony doesn't need anyone else's validation to be real and sacred.

Choosing an Officiant
The person who actually marries you — and shapes everything in between
Critical decision
Plan early ▼
What's actually involved

Your officiant is the person who legally and ceremonially marries you. They write or deliver your ceremony script, manage the pace and tone of the ceremony, guide you through your vows, and handle the legal paperwork — including signing your marriage license and filing it with the county. Choosing the right officiant may have more impact on your ceremony experience than any other single decision.

Your options
Professional celebrant / officiantMost popular non-religious choice

A trained professional whose entire job is creating and delivering wedding ceremonies. They typically meet with you 1–3 times before the wedding, ask extensive questions about your relationship and what matters to you, write a custom script, and rehearse with you beforehand. Professional officiants are often the most reliable choice for couples who want a ceremony that genuinely reflects them. Fees typically range from $400–$1,500 depending on your area and the officiant's experience.

Friend or family member — ordained onlineIncreasingly popular

Anyone can become legally ordained online through the Universal Life Church or similar organizations in minutes — and in most US states, this is fully legal for performing marriages. Having someone who knows and loves you deeply officiate can create an extraordinarily personal ceremony. The risk: public speaking and ceremony structure are different skills than knowing someone well. If you go this route, invest in a script-writing session together and consider a rehearsal. Make sure your state recognizes online ordination — a few states have stricter requirements.

Clergy memberTraditional

A priest, pastor, rabbi, imam, or ordained minister within your faith. The right choice if your faith tradition is important to both of you and you want a ceremony grounded in that structure. Meeting with clergy early is important — many have premarital counseling requirements or specific ceremony structures.

Judge or justice of the peaceCivil baseline

Government-authorized officiants who can perform legally binding civil ceremonies. Available in every county. Often a good option for very simple, short ceremonies — or as the "legal" component for couples who are also having a separate symbolic celebration.

Prep Rooms & The First Look
Getting ready, not seeing each other, and the decision before the ceremony begins
▼
Prep rooms — what venues provide

Most venues include separate preparation rooms for the wedding party and couple — typically a bridal suite and a secondary room for the other partner and their party. These are the spaces where you get ready, where your photographer captures getting-ready moments, and where you wait before the ceremony begins. Ask your venue early: how many prep rooms are available, where they are in relation to the ceremony space, and how movement between them is managed if you don't want to risk crossing paths.

Not seeing each other before the ceremony

The tradition of one partner not seeing the other before the ceremony has roots in superstition (bad luck) and practical history (arranged marriages where the ceremony was the first meeting). Today it's kept for the emotional impact — the reveal at the end of the aisle, with a full audience, is genuinely powerful.

If this matters to you, it's a logistics conversation with your venue, not just a preference. You'll need to think through: which room each party uses, how you get from prep rooms to ceremony start without crossing paths, and who is responsible for keeping the two parties separated. Your venue coordinator and wedding party can manage this — but it needs to be said out loud and assigned explicitly, not assumed.

The first look — a different approach

A first look is a planned private moment between you and your partner — usually outdoors at the venue, just the two of you and your photographer — where you see each other for the first time before the ceremony. It's become genuinely popular for several reasons:

Complete portraits before the ceremonyBiggest practical benefit

With a first look, most formal portraits — couple photos, wedding party, some family — can happen before the ceremony. This frees up the cocktail hour for mingling rather than photos, and means you're actually present at your own celebration.

A private emotional momentOften more emotional than the aisle

Many couples find the first look — just the two of them, no audience — more emotionally resonant than the aisle reveal. You can actually talk to each other, hold each other, take a breath. The aisle moment is still its own thing; it's simply different rather than lesser.

Calmer before the ceremony

Seeing your partner before the ceremony tends to reduce nerves significantly. You've already had your moment; the ceremony becomes something to share with your guests rather than something to get through.

Neither approach is better. The aisle reveal is genuinely moving and worth choosing if it matters to you. The first look is genuinely worth considering if portraits, nerves, or cocktail hour presence is a priority. Discuss it with your photographer early — it affects their entire timeline plan.

Overview — how long does a ceremony actually take?

Most wedding ceremonies run 20–40 minutes from the first processional step to the last recessional. Religious ceremonies often run longer — 45–75 minutes. The breakdown below reflects a typical non-denominational or civil ceremony. Add 5–10 minutes for a unity ceremony, 1–3 minutes per reading, and extra recessional time for larger wedding parties.

The full ceremony, in order
1
Prelude — guests arrive & are seated15–30 minStandard

Soft background music plays as guests find their seats. This sets the emotional atmosphere before anything official begins. Most couples use gentle instrumental music — piano, acoustic guitar, a string quartet, or a curated playlist. Your DJ or musicians start as soon as the first guests arrive.

Playlist approach: A single genre or artist that means something to you. Less expensive, consistent volume, fully controlled.
Live musicians: Strings, harp, acoustic duo. Much higher emotional impact. Requires coordination with your DJ for the transition to the ceremony proper.
Silence / ambient: Some venues and couples prefer quiet. Guests tend to talk more, which can create a warm, social pre-ceremony feel rather than a reverent one.
2
Grandparents escorted to seats1–2 minOptional

If grandparents are attending, an usher (or a member of the wedding party) escorts them to reserved front seats before other family. This is a quiet honor — most guests won't even realize it's a distinct moment, but grandparents absolutely notice and appreciate it.

Why include it: Honors grandparents visibly. Gives them a moment of recognition before the ceremony starts. Establishes that the processional is beginning.
Why skip it: Adds a logistical step. Works best when grandparents are mobile and can walk the aisle. If they're already seated or mobility is a concern, skip and seat them early without fanfare.
Music: Usually same prelude music continues — no separate song needed unless you want to make a moment of it.
3
Immediate family seated2–4 minStandard

Parents and immediate family are escorted to the front reserved rows. The traditional order: family of one partner first, then family of the other — with the last parent seated signaling to guests that the processional is imminent. This is the moment when guests stop socializing and face forward. In traditional setups, the final parent to be seated is often the mother of the person who will enter last.

Standard order: Family of Partner A seated → Family of Partner B seated (last family member seated = ceremony starting signal)
For LGBTQ+ couples / two equal partners: Either order works. Some couples choose who goes first based on whose family is larger, or simply flip a coin. Your DJ will cue music transitions regardless of order.
Dedicated family seating song: Optional upgrade — a separate, more meaningful song plays just for this. Adds emotional weight to the family moment before the wedding party enters. Typically 1–2 minutes.
Divorced or separated parents: If both parents are attending, they can be escorted separately with their own partner/guest, or by different members of the wedding party. Brief coordination beforehand prevents any awkward standing around.
4
Partner A entrance — or waiting at the altar0–2 minOptional (how they enter varies)

In many ceremonies, one partner is already standing at the altar when the processional begins — they entered quietly beforehand with the officiant. This is the traditional "groom waits at the altar" format. But there's a growing and genuinely moving alternative: Partner A walks down the aisle to their own dedicated song before the wedding party enters.

Already at the altar (traditional): Partner A enters from a side room or door with the officiant, takes their place, and watches the processional. No music needed. Most common in traditional religious ceremonies.
Dedicated entrance song (modern upgrade): Partner A walks the full aisle to their own song — just like their partner will. This equalizes the ceremony, gives the first partner their own emotional moment, and often produces a visible emotional reaction when they see their partner for the first time. Increasingly popular. Adds ~1–2 minutes to ceremony length.
Both walk in together: Skip the sequential format entirely — both partners enter simultaneously, side by side. Works beautifully when neither person wants a spotlight moment, or when both want the moment equally. See the Processional card for full detail.
Entered with parents: Partner A can be escorted by one or both parents down the aisle, the same way Partner B traditionally is.

The "dedicated entrance song" option for the first partner has become one of the most emotionally resonant upgrades available. Watching one partner's face when they see the other begin walking toward them — often for the first time all day — is something guests consistently describe as the most powerful moment of the ceremony.

5
Wedding party processional2–4 minStandard

Attendants walk down the aisle, typically in pairs, to a shared song. Traditionally honor attendants (maid/matron of honor, best man) walk last before the main entrance. The wedding party song is usually something elegant and measured — giving each person time to walk without rushing.

One shared song: Most common. Everyone walks to the same piece. Simple, cohesive, easy to coordinate.
Separate songs per attendant or pair: Premium option. Each person or pair walks to a different, meaningful song. Much more personalized — creates individual moments for each attendant. Requires more DJ coordination and a longer processional.
No specific order by gender: Attendants can walk in any pairing — friends with friends, siblings together, or solo. There's no rule requiring gender separation.
Children (ring bearer / flower child / junior bridesmaid): Usually walks just before Partner B. Roles don't need to be gender-matched — any child can carry rings or scatter petals. A few distinctions worth knowing:
  • Ring bearer — traditionally carries the rings (use a dummy set — never real rings on a moving child). Any gender.
  • Flower child — gender-neutral term for the petal-scattering role; fully replaces "flower girl" when that framing doesn't fit.
  • Junior bridesmaid — an older child (typically 8–12) who walks with the wedding party but has a lighter set of responsibilities than adult attendants. Meaningful way to include a younger sibling or cousin who is too old to be a flower child but too young for a full attendant role.
6
Partner B entrance — the main entrance1–2 minStandard

The entrance everyone has been waiting for. Music shifts — often a pause of a beat or two, then the new song begins. Guests stand. This is the most important music decision of the entire ceremony. The song needs to make the person walking feel something, and it needs to hold for the full length of the aisle walk — typically 60–90 seconds of actual walking.

Walking alone: Increasingly popular. A statement of self-determination. Just as beautiful as being escorted.
Escorted by one parent: Traditional. The right side is typically the parent's right arm. Either parent, or either arm — the protocol is less important than the intention.
Escorted by both parents: A beautiful option — partner walks between both parents. Removes any "giving away" dynamic and centers the love of both parents equally.
Escorted by children: If blending a family, having children walk their parent down the aisle creates an extraordinary family formation moment.
Escorted by chosen family: A sibling, close friend, mentor — anyone who holds that place in your life. There is no protocol requiring a biological parent.

When choosing your entrance song, think about length before you think about feeling. Time your aisle walk during the rehearsal. Most couples walk 60–90 seconds. A song that feels emotionally perfect but starts with a 45-second intro means you're already halfway down the aisle before the part you love even begins. Your DJ can create a custom edit that starts at exactly the right moment.

7
Welcome & opening words2–4 minStandard

The officiant welcomes guests, acknowledges the significance of the gathering, and sets the tone for the ceremony. This is usually where the officiant introduces themselves and briefly describes what's about to happen. A great officiant uses this moment to make the room feel warm and present — not to recite boilerplate.

Personal vs. formal: The best opening welcomes reference the couple specifically — how they met, what makes them them. Generic openings lose the room quickly.
Guest acknowledgment: Some officiants acknowledge guests who traveled far, or who represent specific meaning to the couple. A brief nod to this makes guests feel genuinely included.
8
Reading(s)1–3 min eachOptional

A guest reads a poem, passage, or piece of scripture aloud. Typically placed after the welcome and before the vows. Most ceremonies include one or two readings maximum — beyond that, attention starts to drift before the vows even begin.

Why include: Involves a meaningful person who isn't in the wedding party. Adds a pause and emotional texture before the vows.
Why skip: If your ceremony is already full, readings eat time that might better belong to your vows. Nobody leaves a wedding wishing there had been a reading — but many leave wishing the vows had been longer.
9
Unity ceremony5–8 minOptional

A symbolic ritual performed after the opening and before (or after) the vows — sand ceremony, unity candle, handfasting, and more. See the full Unity Ceremonies section for the most common options. Typically placed here in the ceremony, though it can also move to after the vows.

Before vows: The ritual builds emotional momentum going into the vow exchange. Works well when the unity ceremony is brief (sand, candle).
After vows: Maintains the vow exchange as the emotional climax. The unity ceremony then becomes a beautiful "sealing" of what was just promised. Works especially well for handfasting.
10
Vows3–8 minStandard

The legal and emotional heart of the ceremony. Either traditional (repeat after me), written by each partner, or a blend. See the full Vows card for common formats and practical guidance. This is the moment the room goes completely quiet. Even in a crowd of hundreds, a whispered vow can feel utterly private.

11
Ring exchange2–4 minStandard

Rings are presented (by best man, ring bearer, or from a ceremonial pillow) and placed on each other's fingers, usually with a few words spoken. The ring warming alternative — where guests have passed and blessed the rings during the processional — means they arrive charged with the intentions of everyone in the room.

Ring warming integration: If doing a ring warming, your rings travel through the audience during the prelude or a reading, then are returned to the officiant just before this moment.
12
Pronouncement & first kiss1 minStandard

The officiant declares you married. Guests typically cheer or applaud. The first kiss follows — exactly as long (or short) as you want. Don't rush. Give it a breath. This is one of the most photographed seconds of your entire life together.

13
Recessional2–4 minStandard

You walk back up the aisle together — usually to something joyful and celebratory. The wedding party follows. Then guests are dismissed row by row by ushers. This is the exhale. Choose a song that feels like release — something that makes people want to clap, cheer, or cry happy tears on the way out.

Classic choices: Handel's "Hallelujah Chorus," Mendelssohn's "Wedding March," a full-orchestra film score moment.
Modern choices: A pop song that captures pure joy — Stevie Wonder, Ben E. King, anything that makes the room feel like celebration.
Surprise choices: Some couples choose something unexpected — an upbeat indie song, a beloved film theme, something with personal meaning that makes guests laugh or gasp in the best way.
14
Postlude — guests exit5–15 minStandard

Music continues as guests file out. Upbeat and warm. Your DJ keeps energy high so the transition into cocktail hour (or photos) feels seamless, not like a stop-and-start.

The decisions most couples don't realize they're making
Does Partner A get their own entrance song?Growing trend

This is the single most underused upgrade in wedding ceremonies. Giving the first partner their own song — rather than having them already at the altar — creates a second emotional peak before the main entrance. The contrast between the two songs, and the visible emotion on both faces, is frequently described as the most beautiful moment of the day. Talk to your DJ and officiant about adding this. It costs nothing extra and adds roughly 90 seconds.

When does the music change for the main entrance?Critical timing

There's usually a deliberate pause of 1–2 seconds between the last attendant taking their place and the main entrance song beginning. That silence is intentional — it's the room holding its breath. Your DJ knows to build this in. The pause matters as much as the song.

Do guests know when to stand?Overlooked detail

In most ceremonies, guests automatically stand when the main entrance music begins. But in outdoor ceremonies, destination settings, or venues where acoustics are unusual, guests sometimes don't realize it's time. Your officiant can briefly instruct guests: "Please rise as [name/names] enter." Your DJ can also cue this by shifting to a noticeably different musical energy that signals "this is the moment."

Where does the unity ceremony go?Matters more than couples expect

Before vows: builds emotional momentum, but splits the ceremony into three acts (opening → unity → vows) which can feel slightly fragmented. After vows: preserves the vow exchange as the climax, then gives you a beautiful ritual to "seal" the promises you just made. There's no wrong answer, but "after vows" tends to feel more complete emotionally. Discuss with your officiant.

The Processional
Walking down the aisle — one of the most emotional moments of the day
Near universal
Ceremony opening ▼
What's actually involved

The processional is the sequence in which people walk down the aisle before the ceremony begins. It establishes who's involved, signals to guests that the ceremony is starting, and builds emotional anticipation — usually culminating in the entrance of the couple. The entire processional typically takes 5–10 minutes depending on aisle length and the number of people walking.

Traditional processional order — the starting point

This is the format you'll see in most traditional Western ceremonies. It was built around a specific family structure — but every element of it is adaptable, reorderable, or skippable. Think of it as a template, not a script.

Officiant takes their place at the altar
Grandparents are escorted to their seats (if included)
Family of Partner A are escorted and seated
Family of Partner B — traditionally the last family member seated signals the ceremony is about to begin
Partner A enters (from a side door, or walks down the aisle with or without family)
Wedding party walks — attendants, then honor attendants
Ring bearer and flower child (if included — any child, any gender)
Partner B enters last — music shifts, guests stand, hearts stop
Creative alternatives & variations
Both partners walk in togetherGrowing fast

An increasingly popular choice, and a natural fit for many LGBTQ+ couples or anyone who doesn't want one person "entering last" as the traditional focal point. You walk down the aisle side by side, together from the start. Deeply symbolic, and often produces its own distinct emotional moment.

Both partners escorted by their own family or chosen familyInclusive

Each partner is escorted by their own parents, siblings, chosen family, or whoever holds that place in their life. This removes the "giving away" connotation entirely and centers the ceremony on two whole people joining each other — not being transferred from one household to another.

Walk yourself down the aisleIncreasingly common

Walking yourself down the aisle is a powerful statement of self-determination. It's not cold or lonely — it's intentional and confident. Many guests find it quietly moving. If no parent is present, if relationships are complicated, or if you simply want to arrive under your own power, this needs no explanation.

Wedding party in any configurationFlexible

There are no gender requirements for who walks with whom. Attendants can be called whatever feels right — honor attendants, wedding party, people of honor — and they can walk in any pairing or grouping that reflects your actual relationships. The tradition was built around gender separation; your wedding doesn't have to be.

No wedding party at allSimple & intimate

Some couples choose to have only their immediate or chosen family walk in, or just themselves. This simplifies logistics enormously and puts all the focus on the two of you.

Children walk with youFamily-centered

If you're blending families or have children, walking with them — rather than giving them a separate ceremonial role — can be a powerful statement of family formation. This moment often draws the most tears of any in the ceremony.

Music choices

The processional has two distinct music moments: the wedding party walk, and the final partner's entrance. Most couples choose different music for each — something beautiful for the wedding party, then a shift to something more emotionally charged for the entrance itself. Classical pieces (Pachelbel's Canon, Wagner's Bridal Chorus, Beethoven, Handel) are traditional but by no means required. Many couples choose modern songs, movie scores, or pieces that are personally meaningful. The only rule is that whatever plays for your entrance should make you feel something.

The moment your entrance song begins is often when guests rise to their feet and the room changes. If that matters to you, give that song choice serious attention.

The "who gives this person" question

The traditional "who gives this woman" prompt has historical roots in marriage as a property transfer. Many modern couples and officiants choose to skip it entirely, or replace it with something like "Who supports and celebrates this union?" — to which the entire congregation responds "We do." Some couples keep traditional language because it feels meaningful within their faith. For many LGBTQ+ couples and others, the original framing simply doesn't apply — and that's a good reason to rewrite it. There's no wrong answer here, just make sure it's a deliberate choice, not an inherited default.

Readings & Poems
Sharing words that mean something during the ceremony

Personal touch ▼
What's actually involved

A reading is a moment during the ceremony where a guest — often a close friend, family member, or sibling who isn't in the wedding party — reads a passage aloud. It might be a poem, a piece of scripture, a paragraph from a favorite book, or something written specifically for the occasion. Readings typically run 1–3 minutes and appear near the beginning of the ceremony, after the opening remarks and before the vows. Most ceremonies include one or two. Some include none.

What to consider
Scripture / religious textsTraditional

1 Corinthians 13 ("Love is patient, love is kind") is the most-read passage at Christian weddings and is beautiful precisely because of its specificity about what love requires. The Song of Solomon, Ecclesiastes 4:9–12, and Ruth 1:16–17 are also widely used. Jewish ceremonies often include passages from the Torah or Psalms. If religion is part of your ceremony, scripture readings can be deeply grounding.

PoetryUniversal choice

Pablo Neruda, Rumi, e.e. cummings, Mary Oliver, and Wendell Berry are among the most requested poets at secular ceremonies. A poem that genuinely resonates with you is almost always a better choice than a "famous wedding poem" chosen because it seemed appropriate. Ask your officiant or a literary-minded friend for suggestions based on what actually matters to you.

Prose from books, films, or personal writingGrowing trend

A passage from a book you both love, dialogue from a film that means something to you, or a piece written specifically for your wedding by a friend or family member can be profoundly moving. There are no rules here about what's "appropriate." If it's true to you, it belongs.

No readings at allCompletely fine

Many beautiful ceremonies include no readings. If your ceremony is short, intimate, or you simply don't feel drawn to them, skipping readings keeps the focus on your vows and the exchange itself. Nobody leaves wishing there had been a reading.

Tips for managing readings gracefully tap to expand +
  • Choose readers who are comfortable speaking publicly. Being someone you love dearly does not automatically mean they'll be calm at a microphone in front of a crowd. Consider this honestly and kindly.
  • Give readers their passage well in advance — ideally 6–8 weeks before the wedding. Give them a printed copy for the ceremony day, even if they've memorized it.
  • Keep readings to 1–2 minutes. Longer than that and you'll notice guests losing attention before the vows even begin.
  • Practice with the microphone. If your ceremony uses amplified sound, ask your reader to attend the rehearsal so they're comfortable with volume and distance.
  • Avoid requiring a standing ovation moment. Readings work best when they create quiet feeling — not performance pressure on the reader.

Vows & Ring Exchange
The promises at the heart of everything — and how to make them feel real
Universal
The heart of it all ▼
What's legally required

In most US states, the legal requirement is simply verbal consent — some form of "I do" or equivalent agreement to enter marriage. Everything beyond that is personal choice. The specific words of traditional vows are not legally mandated. This means your ceremony can include whatever vows feel true to you, as long as the legal consent element is present.

Vow options explained
Traditional religious vowsEstablished language

The words millions of people have said before you: "I take you to be my lawfully wedded spouse, to have and to hold, from this day forward, for better for worse, for richer for poorer, in sickness and in health, to love and to cherish, until death do us part." These words carry weight precisely because they've been spoken across generations. If this language means something to you — if the tradition itself is part of the meaning — then this is exactly right for you. Don't let trends talk you out of it.

Writing your own vowsMost emotionally powerful

Personal vows — written by each of you, spoken to each other — are consistently described by couples as the most emotionally overwhelming moment of their wedding day. They're also the most commonly cited thing couples wish they'd done when they chose not to. Writing your own vows doesn't require literary talent; it requires honesty. Aim for 1–2 minutes when spoken aloud. Address your partner directly. Make specific promises rather than general ones. If you're nervous, practice out loud — the words sound different spoken than they do on paper.

Blended / semi-custom vowsPopular middle ground

Start with traditional vow language and add personal additions — a specific promise, an inside reference, a line about your particular story. Your officiant can help you craft this seamlessly. The traditional framework provides emotional grounding while the personal additions make it uniquely yours.

Call-and-response vowsInteractive

Your officiant speaks the words and you repeat them phrase by phrase. This is the classic "repeat after me" format. It takes the pressure of memorization off the couple entirely and keeps the pace moving. Works well for couples who are nervous about public speaking or want to be fully present rather than focused on remembering words.

Group vow / guest participationGrowing trend

The officiant turns to the congregation and asks: "Do you — friends and family — promise to support and celebrate this marriage?" Guests respond "We do." It extends the vow-making beyond the couple and draws the entire room into the ceremony in a quietly powerful way. Often combined with traditional or personal vows, not used as a replacement.

Private vows — shared before or afterIntimate choice

Some couples write personal vows to each other but share them privately — during a first look, the night before, or after the ceremony — and use traditional or repeat-after-me vows during the ceremony itself. This keeps the most intimate promises between just the two of you while still giving guests a ceremony with emotional substance.

Writing your own vows — where to start

Start by asking yourself these questions — separately, then share what you've written with each other: What does this person do that nobody else does? What have they taught you about yourself? What kind of partner do you want to become? What specific promises do you actually intend to keep? What do you most want them to know, in front of everyone who loves you both?

Agree on length, tone, and whether you'll share drafts before the wedding. Some couples prefer the surprise; others find sharing drafts helps them match emotional weight and feel more prepared. Neither is wrong.

Aim for 1 to 2 minutes when read aloud. That's roughly 150–250 words. Shorter than you think. Most couples speak faster than normal when nervous, so add a few beats of pause.

The ring exchange

The ring exchange typically follows the vow exchange and takes 3–5 minutes. The rings are presented — by whoever holds them, whether that's the best man, ring bearer, or another person you've chosen — and each partner places the ring on the other's finger and may speak a few words. Ring exchange words can be as simple as "with this ring, I thee wed" or as elaborate as another short vow. Some couples include a meaningful phrase they'll see every time they look at their hands. The order — vows then rings — is traditional, but some ceremonies do rings first, then vows. Your officiant will guide the flow.

Tips for nervous vow readers tap to expand +
  • Print your vows on small cards, not phone screens. Phones can freeze, dim, or feel awkward. A physical card you've practiced with is far more reliable.
  • Look up from the page as much as you can — especially for your most important lines. Eye contact during vows is what people remember.
  • Give yourself permission to cry. Take a breath, smile at your partner, and continue when you're ready. This is not a performance; it's real. Guests understand.
  • Practice out loud at least 5 times before the wedding. The words change when you speak them, and you need to know how they land in your own voice.
  • Keep a tissue in your pocket or bouquet. Your maid of honor or best man can hold one for you. Plan for this.

The First Kiss & Recessional
The moment it all becomes real — and the joyful exit that follows
Universal
Ceremony close ▼
The first kiss

After the pronouncement ("you are now married"), the officiant invites you to share your first kiss as a married couple. This is the moment guests have been anticipating, and for most couples, the emotional culmination of the ceremony. There's no instruction manual — kiss as you naturally would, or not at all if public displays of affection aren't your thing. Either is completely valid; if PDA isn't comfortable for you, a hug or forehead touch is a warm alternative that photographs beautifully. This is worth a brief conversation between you two before the day — your officiant can frame the moment however you prefer. The only real tip: don't rush. Give your photographer and the moment both a full second to breathe.

Variations
Extended dip or theatrical kissCrowd-pleaser

Some couples plan a more dramatic kiss — a dip, a lift, a longer embrace — that plays to the crowd. If this fits your personality, it produces one of the best photos of the day. Practice it once beforehand so it doesn't feel awkward in the moment.

No kiss at allValid choice

Some couples — particularly those with certain religious convictions or simply strong preferences around public affection — choose to skip the kiss entirely, or share a forehead touch or embrace instead. This is entirely valid. Your officiant can phrase the pronouncement accordingly.

"You may now embrace"Warm alternative

Some officiants playfully invite guests to hug each other at this moment rather than prescribing the couple's kiss. Creates a joyful communal moment. Works especially well at small, intimate ceremonies.

The recessional

After the first kiss, the couple walks back up the aisle together — usually to a more upbeat, celebratory song than the processional. The wedding party follows behind, then parents, then guests are dismissed row by row. The recessional typically runs 3–5 minutes and marks the official end of the ceremony. This is your moment to beam, hold hands, and walk out as a married couple for the very first time.

Recessional music

The recessional song should feel like exhaling — like joy released. Handel's "Hallelujah Chorus" and Mendelssohn's "Wedding March" are classic choices. Many couples choose a pop song that captures the celebratory feeling — Stevie Wonder, Ben E. King, or something that makes guests want to clap. The recessional is the moment guests finally relax and celebrate with you, so the song matters more than people give it credit for.

The most important decision: professional handler vs. guest

Hire a professional pet handler. This is not optional if you want to enjoy your ceremony. Ceremony roles — what's possible
Ring bearerMost popular role

Your dog (or confident cat) walks down the aisle carrying a decorative pillow, pouch, or collar attachment with the rings. Use a duplicate or dummy ring set — never the real rings on a moving animal. The moment when an excited dog bounds down the aisle toward the couple is consistently one of the most photographed and most talked-about moments of any ceremony that includes it.

Flower petWorks for cats too

Your pet wears a floral collar, halo, or harness arrangement and walks (or is carried) down the aisle scattering petals or simply walking ahead of the wedding party. This works beautifully for both dogs and cats — cats can be carried in a decorated basket or stroller, which actually photographs wonderfully. For cats, a calm, carrier-comfortable animal is essential. Don't attempt free-roaming aisle walks with cats unless you know your specific cat extremely well. Ensure all flowers used are pet-safe — many common florals (lilies especially) are toxic to cats.

First down the aisle — leads the processionalSets a joyful tone

Your pet leads the entire processional — walking the aisle before the wedding party enters. This works beautifully for confident, social dogs who love attention and don't spook easily in crowds. Attach flowers to their collar or vest. Have a handler they trust at the far end. The energy this creates in the room — laughter, applause, genuine delight — immediately warms the atmosphere before the emotional processional begins. It's a tonal reset that makes guests feel like the celebration is genuinely personal.

Walk down the aisle with youFamily formation moment

Your pet accompanies you down the aisle on a leash — walking beside you or between you and your escort. This works particularly well for a partner walking solo (a dog companion changes the emotional energy of a solo entrance from solitary to deliberate and familial), or for couples entering together with their dog between them as a visible representation of the family unit that already exists. One handler note: someone you trust needs to be positioned at the altar to receive the dog's leash so you can be hands-free for the ceremony.

Witness — legal in 23+ statesGenuinely unique

In over 23 US states and Washington D.C., a pet can legally serve as a witness to a marriage. This includes California, Iowa, Nevada, New York, and others. In states requiring only one witness, your pet can be the sole witness. Contact your county marriage license office to confirm your state's specific requirements. This is more of a meaningful symbolic gesture than a legal powerhouse — but it creates a wonderful ceremony story and a genuinely unique marriage certificate.

Stand beside you at the altarFor the calmest, most bonded pets

For well-trained dogs who are deeply bonded to one partner, standing quietly beside the couple through the vows creates a powerful visual of the existing family being expanded. This requires a genuinely calm, crowd-comfortable dog, a handler standing just off to the side, and your officiant's awareness that a dog is present. Best for outdoor or semi-outdoor ceremonies where there's more space and the acoustics don't create anxiety. Be honest about your dog's temperament here — a dog who starts barking during your vows is a much bigger moment than the one you planned.

Attendant alongside the wedding partyFormally included

List your pet in the ceremony program with an official title: "Dog of Honor," "Best Dog," "Chief Paw Officer." Have them walk with a specific member of the wedding party. Give the handler a matching boutonniere or floral accessory so they look like part of the procession rather than a bystander managing an animal. The formalization of the role — seeing it printed in the program — creates a moment of delight when guests read it before the ceremony begins.

When your pet can't or shouldn't attend

Loving your pet sometimes means not including them. An anxious dog in a crowd of 150 strangers with loud music and applause is not having a good time — and you will spend your ceremony worried about them rather than present in your own wedding. Age, anxiety history, reactivity around strangers, noise sensitivity, and venue restrictions are all legitimate reasons to keep a pet home. This is the right choice if it applies to your animal.

There are meaningful alternatives that honor your pet without distressing them: framed photos of your pet on the welcome or memorial table, custom pet illustrations on the cake topper or signage, your pet's name on a signature cocktail ("The Golden Retriever" or "Whiskers' Old Fashioned"), pet-themed favors guests take home, and your pet featured prominently in engagement photos used throughout the décor. These approaches honor the bond without compromising your pet's wellbeing or your ability to be present.

Attire, safety & practical prep
What to dress them inComfort first

A floral collar or bow tie is the least invasive and most reliable option — both can be attached and removed quickly if your pet becomes uncomfortable. Full tuxedos, tutus, and elaborate costumes photograph beautifully but should only be used if your specific pet tolerates clothing without stress (some dogs hate it; some cats will perform full exorcism-level protests). If in doubt, go simpler. The animal's expression matters more in photos than the outfit — a relaxed dog in a bow tie beats an anxious dog in a tux every time.

Flower safetyCritical for cats

Many common wedding flowers are toxic to pets — especially cats. Lilies (all varieties) are severely toxic to cats and can cause kidney failure. Tulips, daffodils, azaleas, baby's breath, and sweet peas are also problematic. Safe options include roses, sunflowers, orchids, snapdragons, and lisianthus. Tell your florist your pet will be present; they can design pet-safe floral accessories specifically.

The day-of prepLogistics matter

Have someone exercise your dog before the ceremony to reduce anxiety and excess energy. Ensure they've eaten, had water, and used the bathroom within an hour of their role. If there's a rehearsal, bring your pet and run through their moment. Familiar smells and familiar people reduce stress significantly. Have a backup plan named explicitly: if [pet name] seems overwhelmed, [handler name] will take them to [specific location]. State this out loud to your handler and officiant.

Where these traditions actually come from

The white wedding dress was not a long-standing tradition — it was a fashion choice by Queen Victoria in 1840, who wore white satin rather than the silver dress she was expected to wear. Before 1840, brides simply wore their best dress, in any color. White became a status symbol (it showed you could afford to keep a dress clean), then a tradition, then an expectation — all within about 60 years. The classic black tuxedo traces to 1865, when the future King Edward VII commissioned a shorter evening jacket as a less formal alternative to the tailcoat. It crossed to the US via Tuxedo Park, New York, where American elites adopted it, creating the name. Neither "white dress" nor "black tuxedo" is ancient. Both are Victorian-era fashion choices that became cultural defaults.

The dress — history, alternatives & what's changing
White or ivory gown — the classicStill the most popular

The overwhelming majority of wedding gowns sold in the US are white, ivory, or champagne. Silhouettes in 2024–2025 range from minimalist crepe sheaths to dramatic ball gowns. Key trends include off-the-shoulder necklines, detachable trains (one dress, two looks), feather and fringe details for dramatic settings, and clean architectural minimalism. If you love the white dress and it feels right, wear it without apology — there's nothing dated about wanting the thing you've wanted. The trend culture around weddings can make couples feel like they need to do something "different" when what they actually want is the classic version. Classic is not a lesser choice.

ColorFastest growing trend

Colored wedding dresses are growing significantly. Soft blush, powder blue, and lavender are the most popular entry points. Bolder couples are choosing deep forest green, burgundy, and black. Some cultures have always worn color — red is traditional in Chinese and South Asian weddings; gold is significant in many West African traditions. For Western couples choosing color, the effect is often that the dress becomes a much stronger reflection of personality than a white gown. It photographs differently too — tends to stand out more against venues and florals.

Bridal pantsuit or tuxedoStrong growing category

A tailored white or ivory pantsuit, blazer-and-trouser set, or bridal tuxedo is now a fully mainstream choice. More comfortable than a ballgown for dancing and for long reception nights, more practical for outdoor weddings, and genuinely striking in photographs. Works beautifully for second weddings, elopements, minimalist aesthetic couples, and any person for whom a dress has never felt right. The bridal suit industry has grown substantially — major bridal retailers now carry full collections. Custom tailoring is also increasingly accessible.

Jumpsuit or playsuitReception favorite

Some couples wear a gown for the ceremony and change into a jumpsuit or playsuit for the reception — comfortable for dancing, distinctive for photos, and allows a genuine outfit "moment" that guests respond to. Others choose the jumpsuit as their primary wedding look. Particularly popular for outdoor, destination, and casual-to-semi-formal weddings.

Cultural dressRich & meaningful

Lehenga choli, hanbok, qipao/cheongsam, kimono, kente cloth, sari — many couples choose cultural attire that honors their heritage, either for the full wedding or as a reception change. For interfaith or multicultural couples, combining elements of two traditions in one look or having one outfit per ceremony is increasingly common and genuinely beautiful. A skilled tailor who works in your specific cultural tradition is worth seeking out even if it means traveling or ordering remotely.

Two-outfit approachGrowing fast

Wearing one outfit for the ceremony and changing into something different for the reception is increasingly common — and for good reason. The ceremony outfit can be more formal, more traditional, or more fragile; the reception outfit can be more comfortable, more expressive, or simply more fun. The "outfit change reveal" is a genuine crowd moment if your DJ announces it.

The suit, tuxedo & beyond — for grooms and non-dress-wearing partners
Classic black tuxedoStill the gold standard for formal

Black or midnight blue jacket with satin lapel, matching trousers, white dress shirt, bow tie, black patent shoes. The tuxedo says formal without requiring explanation — it reads immediately to every guest. Appropriate for black-tie or elevated semi-formal weddings. The modern slim-fit tuxedo has replaced the boxy 1990s version almost entirely; sharper silhouettes and varied lapel styles (peak, notch, shawl) allow significant personalization within the classic format. Worth renting unless you attend 3+ black-tie events per year.

Navy, charcoal, or grey suitMost versatile choice

A well-tailored navy or charcoal suit is the most universally correct choice across formality levels — works for everything from outdoor garden ceremonies to hotel ballrooms. Navy in particular photographs beautifully and pairs with nearly every wedding color palette. Grey suits range from light (casual, summer) to charcoal (formal, year-round). A three-piece suit (add waistcoat) elevates any of these without requiring a tuxedo. Unlike a black tuxedo, a navy or charcoal suit worn at your wedding can be worn again — this matters if you're budget-conscious.

Colored suits2024 trend — growing fast

Forest green, plum, rust, dusty blue, sage, burgundy — colored suits are one of the biggest men's wedding trends of 2024–2025. The color often connects to the wedding palette (a sage suit paired with sage bridesmaid dresses creates a cohesive visual narrative). Bold choices like bright blue or deep plum require more confidence but produce genuinely distinctive photos. Softer palettes (blush, stone, light grey) read as modern-romantic. One practical note: colored suits are almost never wearable again; factor this into your buy-vs-rent decision.

Linen, cotton or textured suitsOutdoor & warm-weather essential

For outdoor, beach, destination, and warm-weather weddings, linen or cotton suits are the correct choice — breathable, comfortable, and with a relaxed elegance that a wool suit can't achieve at 85°F. Off-white or tan linen particularly suits beachside or tropical settings. The trade-off: linen wrinkles. Embrace it as part of the aesthetic or have the suit steamed on-site immediately before the ceremony.

Cultural and heritage attireRich & distinctive

Kilts with Highland dress (jacket, waistcoat, sporran) — particularly for Scottish or Irish heritage celebrations — remain one of the most striking looks at any wedding. Sherwani (South Asian embroidered jacket worn over churidar trousers) is standard for South Asian ceremonies. Barong Tagalog for Filipino weddings. Agbada for West African ceremonies. Cultural heritage attire connects the wedding to a lineage that a suit cannot — and for many couples, particularly first-generation Americans, it represents an intentional honoring of roots.

Matching or coordinated partners — both in similar looksLGBTQ+ & beyond

Two partners in matching suits, matching dresses, or coordinated versions of the same look (same color, different silhouette) creates powerful visual cohesion in photos and ceremony. Two brides in white creates a genuinely striking aisle moment. Two grooms in tuxedos — matching or complementary — photographs beautifully and reads clearly as intentional rather than accidental. The "matching" look is not limited to same-sex couples; some opposite-sex couples also choose coordinated rather than contrasting looks.

The wedding party — from uniform to individual
Matching — identical outfitsClean visual cohesion

All bridesmaids in the same dress and color; all groomsmen in the same suit. Photographs cleanly. Easy to coordinate. The couple chooses; attendants wear. The potential downside: one dress that looks perfect on one body type may not flatter others. If you go this route, choose something versatile and keep comfort in mind — your attendants will wear this for 8+ hours.

Same color, different stylesMost popular modern approach

Give attendants a color (dusty rose, sage green, navy) and a fabric swatch, then let each person choose a style that works for their body and preferences. The result photographs as cohesive (the color ties it together) while feeling genuinely individual. Attendants are more comfortable; you spend less time worrying about flattering every person. This approach has largely replaced identical bridesmaid dresses at modern weddings.

Works beautifully at outdoor, garden, and bohemian-aesthetic weddings. Less cohesive in formal photos — make sure your photographer knows this is intentional so they frame it as a feature rather than a coordination failure.

No dress code for the wedding partyMinimal & freeing

Some couples — particularly at small, intimate weddings — simply ask their wedding party to wear something they feel confident and happy in, with no color or style requirement. The wedding party looks like a group of real people who love you. Can feel wonderfully authentic. Less visually structured in formal photos.

Mixed-gender wedding party looksIncreasingly common

When your wedding party includes people of different genders, a cohesive visual approach matters more than matching gender to dress/suit. Common solutions: everyone in the same color in their preferred silhouette (dresses, suits, jumpsuits); everyone in suits; a deliberately coordinated mix where suits and dresses are in complementary shades of the same palette. Unity Ceremonies

A unity ceremony is a symbolic ritual performed during the wedding — separate from the vows — that represents the joining of two lives. They're optional, deeply meaningful when chosen thoughtfully, and completely skippable if they don't resonate.

What Is a Unity Ceremony?
Understanding why they exist, when they work, and when to skip them
~45–55% include one
Read this first ▼
The purpose

A unity ceremony creates a physical, visible act that represents the emotional and spiritual joining of two people. Where vows are words spoken, a unity ritual is something done — something guests can watch, something you can take home. They typically last 5–10 minutes and are placed after the vow exchange but before the pronouncement. Many couples use them to involve children from previous relationships, honor parents, or include a cultural tradition meaningful to their families.

When they work best

Unity ceremonies are most effective when the ritual genuinely means something to you — when you chose it intentionally, not because it seemed like something weddings have. They work beautifully for blending families (children can participate), for honoring cultural or religious traditions, and for couples who want something tangible to take home. They can also give shorter, simpler ceremonies a sense of substance and ceremony without adding complex structure.

They're least effective when chosen to fill time or because you felt like you "should" have one. A thoughtful officiant can help you make this call honestly.

Practical notes

Most unity ceremonies require props or supplies that need to be set up before the ceremony — candles, sand vials, a box, cords. Coordinate with your venue and officiant about who manages this setup. If you're including children or family members in the ritual, practice it with them beforehand so no one is surprised on the day. And if you plan to take the unity object home (sand vessel, wine box, sealed letter), make sure someone is designated to handle it safely after the ceremony.

Unity Candle Ceremony

Most popular
Classic choice ▼
A little history

The unity candle ceremony has roots in early Christian marriage traditions, where a candle symbolized the presence of God in the union. The modern three-candle format — two individual taper candles and one large central candle — became widely popular in the United States in the 1970s and 1980s, partly through television coverage of high-profile weddings. It's now the most widely recognized unity tradition across religious and secular ceremonies alike, frequently used in Catholic, Protestant, and non-denominational ceremonies.

How it works
Two smaller taper candles are pre-lit — often by the mothers of each partner at the start of the ceremony, representing their individual families and the life they've each given
After the vow exchange, each partner takes their individual candle
Together, they use both flames to light a single large center candle — symbolizing their two lives merging into one
Importantly, the individual candles remain lit — representing that each person retains their individual identity within the marriage
The unity candle is typically taken home and displayed or relit on anniversaries

Handfasting
The ancient Celtic origin of "tying the knot"
Growing fast
Celtic tradition ▼
Where "tying the knot" comes from

Handfasting is a centuries-old Celtic and Norse marriage ritual in which the couple's joined hands were bound together with cord or ribbon as a symbol of their union. In medieval Scotland and Ireland, a handfasting ceremony could constitute a legally binding trial marriage for "a year and a day." If both parties still consented after that period, the marriage became permanent. The phrase "tying the knot" comes directly from this practice. Today it's used symbolically in both religious and secular ceremonies across many traditions.

How it works

You join hands, facing each other. The officiant (or a designated loved one) winds a cord, ribbon, or collection of ribbons around your clasped hands in a figure-eight or infinity pattern, while speaking words about the significance of the binding. Each cord is typically a chosen color with symbolic meaning. At the end, the cord forms a loose knot — representing the bonds of your marriage. You can then carefully slip your hands out while maintaining the knot, which is preserved as a keepsake.

Wine Box / Whiskey Box Ceremony
A time capsule of love — to be opened years from now
Popular keepsake
Future-focused ▼
What's actually involved

Before or during the ceremony, each partner writes a love letter to the other — sealed in an envelope, unread by their partner. Both letters are placed in a box along with a bottle of wine, whiskey, or another meaningful bottle. The box is sealed (often with a hammer and nails, or a lock) during the ceremony. You agree together when to open it — your 5th anniversary, your 10th, or "if we ever go through something really hard." The letter represents who you were on your wedding day; reading it years later is described by couples who've done it as one of the most profound experiences of their marriage.

Variations
"Break glass in case of emergency" versionPractical wisdom

Some couples intend the box specifically for a difficult moment in their marriage — "if we ever feel like giving up, we open this." The letters then read as love notes from your best day, speaking directly to your hardest day. Deeply moving as a concept, and a genuine commitment to working through difficulty.

Guest letters includedCommunity

Ask guests to write a note of advice or a wish on a card at the reception. These are sealed and placed in the box alongside your personal letters. When you open the box years later, you read dozens of messages from the people who celebrated you — some of whom may no longer be with you.

Bourbon barrel ceremonyRegional tradition

Popular in the American South, each partner pours whiskey from their own bottle into a small shared oak barrel, which is then sealed and stored. You open it on your 5th or 10th anniversary and share the now-blended, barrel-aged spirit. The whiskey that's aged and mellowed together is a metaphor that speaks for itself.

What's involved

What's involved

During or after dinner, selected guests are invited to speak. The traditional order was best man first, then maid of honor — but you can invite anyone to speak, in any order, using whatever title feels right for them. A little history

Wedding cakes trace to ancient Rome, where a wheat loaf was broken over one partner's head as a fertility symbol. Victorian England popularized the tiered white cake after Queen Victoria's 1840 wedding. The shared first-slice tradition symbolized the couple's first joint act of providing for each other. The top tier was historically saved for their first child's christening — today most couples save it for their first anniversary.

The cake smash — have this conversation

The cake smash (shoving cake in your partner's face) is funny when both people are expecting it, and mortifying when one isn't. Make sure you're both genuinely in on whatever happens. Being surprised with a face full of frosting in front of your grandmother is a very different experience than knowing it's coming and playing along. Have the conversation privately before the wedding day.

Alternatives
Dessert bar instead of cakePopular

Real reservations worth knowing

Calling single guests to the floor publicly can put people on the spot — those who are recently divorced, sensitive about relationship status, dealing with loss, or simply uncomfortable being singled out. The origin — worth knowing

The garter toss has medieval European origins rooted in marriage consummation rituals — guests would sometimes be present outside or inside the chamber, and pieces of clothing were taken as lucky tokens. By the Renaissance, a public "race for the garter" existed at some weddings. The modern version — one partner retrieves the garter, tosses it to single male guests — likely emerged in 1950s American culture, influenced by burlesque entertainment. By 1960, magazines were calling it a "tradition." The ritual was built around a very specific heterosexual, gendered script — and most people who include it today have no idea where it came from.

The honest feelings conversation

This tradition deserves a real, private conversation between both of you — before the wedding, before the planning meeting, before any decisions are made. Not a quick "do you want to do that?" but a genuine "how do we each actually feel about this?"

Many people feel genuinely uncomfortable with the public retrieval — particularly with family present, with children in the room, or simply because it's an intimate act performed in front of an audience. That discomfort is completely valid and requires no justification. Either partner may feel this way — with the act itself, or with the pressure of making it look entertaining. And some guests — elderly relatives, people with young children — quietly dread watching. For LGBTQ+ couples, the tradition's rigid gender script often doesn't map onto the relationship — many same-sex couples choose to skip or reimagine it. But like every tradition in this guide, it's simply an option: include it if it feels right, skip it if it doesn't, or adapt it into something that works for you both.

If either of you feels a quiet "meh" or hesitation, that's probably your answer. A wedding DJ who respects your day will ask what you'd like — not nudge you toward any particular tradition.

The Anniversary Dance
Celebrating love that's already stood the test of time
Underused gem
Highly recommended ▼
What's involved

All couples are invited to the dance floor for a slow song. Your DJ calls out lengths of time — "if you've been together less than a year, please be seated... less than five years... less than ten years..." This continues until only one pair remains — the longest-together couple at your wedding. They receive your bouquet, a round of applause, or simply the honor. The whole moment runs 5–8 minutes and produces unexpected emotion almost every time.

Why this works

This dance does something almost no other reception moment does: it honors the guests. The couple married 47 years becomes the celebrity of the room. Younger couples lean on each other. The crowd watching sees what love looks like across decades. It's consistently described as one of the most emotional moments of the night — and it comes out of nowhere for people who weren't expecting to feel something.

A note on inclusive framing

Frame this warmly and inclusively. "If you're here with someone you love tonight" rather than strictly "married couples only" makes the dance feel welcoming to every guest rather than accidentally exclusive or painful for someone going through a difficult season.

Last Dance vs. Last Song — they're not the same thing
Understanding the difference changes how you plan the final moments of your night
Critical distinction
Read this carefully ▼
The distinction most couples miss
The last dance
A moment you participate in

The last dance is something you do — a specific, intentional dance between you and your partner (or you and your guests) that marks the emotional close of the celebration. It might be slow and private, or it might be a high-energy floor-filler with everyone around you. But it's a planned, felt experience — not just a song playing while people get their coats.

The last song
The music that closes the room

The last song is what your DJ plays as the final track of the night — often during or just after the send-off, while guests are filing out, grabbing favors, or saying their goodbyes. It sets the mood for the room emptying. It can be the same as your last dance song, or it can be something different — a high-energy closer, a nostalgic crowd favorite, or whatever the room needs in that moment.

These two things can be the same song — but they don't have to be, and understanding the difference lets you make a real decision about each one rather than conflating them into "the last song."

Last dance formats — what's possible
Slow, private couple's last danceIntimate & beautiful

After the send-off, guests have gone. You come back inside. The DJ plays one final song — just for you — in an empty or nearly empty venue with low lights. No audience, no pressure, no performance. Many couples describe this as the single most meaningful moment of their entire wedding day. It requires planning: tell your DJ and venue in advance, make sure the space stays lit (even dimly), and that the venue coordinator knows to stay. It adds roughly 5 minutes but costs nothing extra.

High-energy last dance with everyoneCrowd favorite

The opposite approach: your last dance is the entire floor, together, for one final high-energy song before you wave and walk out. Your DJ builds to a peak — the most crowd-pleasing song of the night, everyone moving, energy at maximum — then you make your exit in the middle of the celebration. This works brilliantly for couples who love dancing and want the night to end not with a quiet moment but with a burst of pure joy.

Announced slow last dance — couple only, then openStructured & memorable

Your DJ announces: "This is the final song of the evening — and we're starting it as a couple's dance for [your names]." You take the floor. Guests watch, phones out. After 60–90 seconds, your DJ invites all couples (or all guests) to join you for the final moments. A beautiful structured ending that gives you your private-feeling moment within a public celebration.

Slow first dance song as your last danceFull circle moment

Some couples choose to end the night with the same song they danced their first dance to — a deliberate full-circle moment that bookends the entire reception. It often lands with much more emotional weight than the first time, because of everything that has happened in between. Your DJ makes a brief, warm announcement before playing it.

Skip a formal last dance entirelyPerfectly valid

Some couples prefer not to have a designated "last dance" moment at all — they want the dancing to feel organic and unceremonious right through the end. Your DJ simply plays out the night with great music, fades the final song as the venue closes, and you make your exit whenever feels right. No announcement, no spotlight, no pressure.

Choosing your last song — what to think about

The last song is different from every other song choice because it's the one guests will carry home with them. Whatever plays as people hug, gather their things, and walk out the door is the emotional note the night ends on — and it lingers. Some couples want that to be euphoric and high-energy. Others want it to be warm and nostalgic. A few want it to be quietly romantic. None of these is wrong.

What doesn't work well: a slow, winding ballad that gradually fades out while guests awkwardly stand around. Energy needs either a clear crescendo and release (high-energy closer) or a graceful, intentional quiet (planned slow song with an announced ending). Ask your DJ what they've seen work for receptions similar in feel to yours. They've watched hundreds of nights end and know what lands.

The private last dance — how to actually make it happen

Step 1: Tell your DJ, venue coordinator, and photographer in advance that you want to come back in after the send-off for a private last dance.

Step 2: Assign someone — a coordinator, a trusted friend — to be ready to herd guests out efficiently when the time comes, so you're not waiting 15 minutes while people linger.

Step 3: Let your photographer know. This produces some of the most iconic photos of the entire wedding — just the two of you, alone, in a beautiful venue, after the whole night is over. They should absolutely stay for it.

Step 4: Pick the song in advance and tell your DJ. In the moment, you will not be thinking clearly enough to make this decision. Plan it now.

The friendship / love circle — a closer look

The friendship circle is growing rapidly as an alternative to or addition to a traditional send-off, particularly among couples in their late 20s and 30s who have tight communities of friends. The reason it works so powerfully is that it briefly transforms your wedding from a celebration of you into a celebration by everyone — your people, gathered in a circle, all present at once. In an era where it's increasingly rare to have everyone you love in the same room at the same time, that visual — a circle of faces you've loved across your whole life — can be overwhelming in the best possible way.

Song choice matters. Something slow, familiar, and singable works best. The goal is that guests feel present and connected, not that they're watching a performance. "I Can't Help Falling in Love," "Somewhere Over the Rainbow," "What a Wonderful World," or something deeply personal to your community are all common choices. Your DJ should make the announcement warm and specific — not a generic "gather round" but something that actually tells guests what's happening and why.

Combining elements — the most memorable endings do more than one thing

The most talked-about reception endings usually combine two elements: a communal moment (friendship circle, final toast, candle lighting) followed by a high-energy or ceremonial close (send-off, confetti, or a surprise song). This gives guests both an emotional moment and a physical release before they leave. Think of it as the ceremony structure applied to your ending: something meaningful, then something joyful.

Frequently asked questions
Do we have to do everything in a specific order?
No. There's a general flow that works well — entrance, dances, dinner, speeches, cake, open dancing, tosses — but your DJ will help you build a timeline that works for your specific venue, catering setup, and priorities. Very few things are truly locked in place.
How long should our reception be?
The dinner-and-dancing reception (after cocktail hour) typically runs 4–5 hours. Total event time including ceremony and cocktail hour is often 5–7 hours — many receptions with a full cocktail hour exceed 5 hours of combined time. Talk to your DJ about the flow you want and build your timeline from there.
Will guests notice if we skip traditions?
Generally, no. Guests notice energy, joy, and how welcome they feel. Very few people leave a wedding thinking "they didn't do a garter toss." They do leave thinking about whether they laughed, danced, cried, and felt part of something meaningful.
How many traditions should we include?
There's no target number. Include the moments that genuinely matter to at least one of you. Let go of the ones you're doing because you feel like you "should." More traditions means more time away from open dancing. Your DJ can help you weigh the tradeoffs honestly.
We're an LGBTQ+ couple — how do we navigate traditions that feel designed for someone else?

By day of week — what actually changes
Friday Best day/price tradeoff

Venue discount: 10–30% vs. Saturday. Venues discount Fridays because demand is lower — couples and their guests are typically still working. Sunday Good option — one caveat

Venue discount: 10–20% vs. Saturday. Sunday works well for guests who don't have long drives home — local weddings rarely see meaningful attendance drops on Sundays, especially for afternoon or early evening ceremonies. The main caveat: a Sunday that falls on a holiday weekend (Memorial Day, Labor Day, Fourth of July) is often charged at Saturday rates — the venue's calendar is in high demand regardless of day. Like Fridays, many non-venue vendors charge the same rate on Sundays — but some DJs, photographers, and coordinators discount off-peak days, so it's worth asking each vendor directly.

Weekday (Mon–Thu) Biggest venue savings, most attendance friction

Venue discount: 30–40% vs. Saturday. Thursday tends to be the cheapest weekday nationally per The Knot data, though the exact percentage varies by year and source. The tradeoff is guest attendance: couples who work with your venue, caterer, and entertainment on a Thursday are investing in a date many guests may not be able to make. Works best for micro-weddings (under 40 guests), destination-style celebrations, or couples whose guest lists are mostly retired or have schedule flexibility. Some vendors — DJs and photographers especially — may offer weekday discounts since demand for a Tuesday or Thursday slot is genuinely low. Worth asking. Florists and catering labor typically don't vary by day. The largest savings reliably come from venue pricing.

By season — what the STL market looks like
Peak season: May–October Highest demand, highest venue pricing

Spring and early fall are the most sought-after months in the STL market — mild weather, outdoor ceremony conditions, and the widest vendor availability. June and September see the most bookings nationally. Venues are priced at their highest. Popular vendors (photographers especially) book 9–12 months out during this window. If you're set on a peak-season Saturday, booking early is the main lever — there's little room to negotiate pricing when a venue is already 80% sold for that date.

Shoulder season: April, October–November Best balance of weather and value

April in the STL area can be unpredictable (cold snaps are possible through mid-April), but late April and October are genuinely beautiful months for weddings and carry lower venue pricing than peak summer. November pricing drops noticeably after the first two weekends — mid-to-late November is one of the better value windows in this market if you're flexible on season. National data suggests November weddings cost somewhat below the annual average — an approximation, not an STL-specific figure.

Off-season: January–March, mid-November–December Deepest discounts, some real tradeoffs

January–March are the slowest months nationally — only 23% of weddings happen November through March per The Knot 2025 data, and January accounts for just 2%. Winter weddings nationally average somewhat less than summer weddings — one source (WithJoy, 2025) cites roughly $2,000 — though STL-specific savings will vary and this figure comes from national aggregates, not local data. Venues that have open January–February dates will often negotiate — upgrades, complimentary add-ons, or reduced minimums are common asks that work during slow periods. Tradeoffs: STL winters are genuinely cold and can include ice/snow events that affect guest travel. Outdoor ceremonies are essentially off the table. Indoor-only venues with good heating infrastructure are your realistic options. Some vendors (florists especially) have lower flower costs in winter; others charge the same year-round.

December 31 exception Peak pricing despite off-season month

New Year's Eve is one of the highest-demand nights of the year for venues, catering staff, and entertainment. Vendors who might otherwise offer off-season pricing will charge peak or above-peak rates for December 31. If you're drawn to a NYE wedding for the celebration feel, budget accordingly — it prices like a peak Saturday regardless of the calendar month.

The most common worry about off-peak days is attendance. For local-heavy guest lists (which describes most STL-area weddings), the real-world impact is modest. What a wedding DJ actually handles

A wedding DJ manages the entire flow of your reception — announcing every moment, cueing your photographer before key events, coordinating with catering on dinner timing, handling microphone handoffs for speeches, reading the room throughout the night, and making in-the-moment decisions that keep the evening on track. The difference between a budget DJ and an experienced professional isn't just sound quality — it's the arc of your entire evening.

A question every couple should ask: will you know your DJ before signing?

This is one of the most important but least-asked questions in DJ shopping. With some multi-DJ companies, the specific DJ assigned to your wedding may not be confirmed until weeks before your event. Complete Weddings + Events, for example, notes on their own website that their DJ typically contacts you approximately 21 days before the event — and their own FAQ acknowledges that with larger DJ companies, "you may find your event assigned a DJ that you're not familiar with." Reviews of Complete frequently use language like "the DJ I was assigned was great" — confirming this is how the model works in practice.

This approach has real advantages: built-in backup coverage, consistent company-wide training, and bundled pricing across multiple services. The tradeoff is that the relationship you build during planning may be with a coordinator or scheduling system, and the DJ who performs may be someone you've never spoken with until close to the date.

With solo or boutique operators, the person you meet during consultation is typically the person who performs. They learn your story, your preferences, and your priorities over months of direct communication — not in a call three weeks out.

Neither model is wrong — know which one you're choosing. Ask explicitly: "Will I know my DJ's name when I sign the contract? Is that the same person I'll communicate with throughout planning?"
